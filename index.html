Parties affected, Pollutants, Environmental Spheres

What

Overview of crisis, pollutants and impacts

Synopsis of crisis; Cuba experiencing severe water crisis leading to scarcity and contamination. Development of issue dating back to around 1959.

  • Neglected infrastructure and waste management causes pollutants and sewage waste

Impact on Hydrosphere:

  • Increase of contamination of surface and groundwater
  • Pipe corrosion introduces dissolved metals to hydrosphere

Where

Impacted areas, evidence of impact, future aspect

Entire island of Cuba impacted:

  • Major urban areas; Havana, and Manzanillo (Granma Province)
  • Large problem in Havana stems from aquifer beneath Almendares River
  • In Granma province, approximately 24% of residents (193,000 people) do not have access to pipe delivered water

Water bodies:

  • Cauto River Basin (longest river in Cuba) originating from foothills of Sierra Maestra Mountain (760m above sea level) and enters Caribbean Sea. Located in eastern part of Cuba
  • Bays and beaches, recalled as dirty by local residents

Future aspect:

  • Officials predict core water problems will continue to flow through 2025
  • Climate change anticipated to worsen weather events
  • Projected increase in tourism to 10 million visitors yearly by 2030 expected to strain Cuban water crisis

When

Timeline of issue and ongoing development

  • Before 1959 (Colonial Rule) original water and sewage systems installed by Spanish
  • Post 1959 (Five decades of high neglect) Little maintenance done on infrastructure for the next 50 years since revolution, causing further deterioration
  • 1981 afterwards; Increase in drought events in Cuba
  • 1990s-Early 2000s; Water disruption caused by governmental issues combined with geomorphic conditions
  • 2000 (Government downplay) Cuban government reported 93% of Cubans access to improved drinking water differed from independent studies and perceived water quality
  • 2004-2005; Period identified as worst drought event in Cauto River basin within 1981-2023 timeframe
  • 2024; Acceptable government efforts, treatment plant installations and conservation plans

Why

Root causes, broader forces

  • Natural causes; Geography and climate, island nation without large rivers. Climate seasonally humid with maritime influence relies heavily on rainfall, prone to droughts
  • Human factors; Obsolete and deteriorating infrastructure with long-term neglect

The broader forces that contribute also include political and economic factors, along with tourism strains.

How

How did it unfold? Talk about the sequence of events, process, or mechanisms.

  • Contamination process; Neglected sewage systems leads to decline and leaks, causing over 40% of pumped water to be lost and contaminated, allowing bacteria to enter.
  • Climate impact; Meteorological droughts lead to hydrological droughts, reduced water in streams and lakes. Lower altitude means longer and more intense droughts (Cauto River basin)
  • Corrosion in pipes; Electrochemical process, pipes react with water and dissolved substances, release of metal ions into water supply
  • Frequent power outages; interrupt electricity for water pump usage through hydraulic network, causes malfunctions for water supply system

Pollutants Involved

Types of pollutants and their risk

  • Contaminated drinking water and raw sewage can inflict gastro-intestinal problems often accompanied by diarrhea
  • Water stored in household cisterns may be contaminated, which can contribute to breeding of mosquitos. Dengue and zika outbreaks (mosquito-borne diseases) may be reported
  • Faecal contamination may lead to pathogens due to sewage origination
  • Acidity in the air, more particulate quantity, and N2O and SO2 concentrations

Journalistic Account

The small island of Cuba has been dealing with a concerning water crisis dating back to before 1959. With its deteriorating infrastructure, Cuba has been facing issues ranging from drinking water contamination, sewage pipe corrosion, as well as drought intensity. Extreme droughts were reported affecting croplands and water availability. In 2008 Cuba became prey to four hurricanes in succession (Fay, Gustav, Ike, Paloma) and damaged 647,111 houses, with 13% of them being completed destroyed. There is also the increase of saline intrusion into the country's groundwater, which puts agriculture at high risk of contamination. Independent studies consistently reveal that the perceived quality of drinking water among Cuban residents and tourists is poor. The main anthropogenic cause is the infrastructure, as when water arrives through municipal systems, it is often under low pressure, allowing bacteria and other foreign material to enter distribution. As reported by the National Institute of Hydraulic Resources (INRH), in early 2025, only 61.2% of the population had access to "risk-free" drinking water services, defined as being available when needed, and free from faecal and chemical contamination. According to several sources, Hepatitis A, typhoid, polio, and cholera are among the diseases that spread through drinking water. Cuba has faced cholera outbreaks in the past, with significant cases reported in 2012 and 2015. The Ministry of Science, Technology, and the Environment (CITMA) announced a national plan in 2018 called "Tarea Vida", which included upgrading water infrastructure and maintenance to slow down the effects of climate change impact. The INRH reported installing 241 kilometers of new water supply networks, along with 32 water treatment plants in 2024. While tourism will offer economic gains, an increase in tourism is likely to increase water pollution, further straining the drinking water system.

Fact vs Fiction

  • Fact: Diseases like cholera, hepatitis A, typhoid, and polio have been linked to contaminated drinking water in Cuba.
  • Fiction: Cuba's government provides safe water access to nearly everyone. Reality: Government statistics conflict with independent research showing much lower safe access rates.
  • Fact: Cuba's outdated pipes corrode, releasing harmful metals like iron copper and lead into drinking water.
  • Fiction: Tourism only benefits Cuba's economy without impacting water supplies. Reality: Projected growth expected to strain water resources a lot more.
  • Fact: Climate change is worsening Cuba's water crisis, increasing droughts, hurricanes, and saline intrusion in aquifers.
